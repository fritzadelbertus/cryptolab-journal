Introduction: Motivation and Initial Plan

June 18, 2026

Motivation

Hello, my name is Fritz, and I am a cryptography researcher.

CryptoLab began with a simple question: what would my ideal research environment look like if I built it myself?

Much of my work revolves around implementing cryptographic protocols, experimenting with mathematical ideas through code, and writing research papers in LaTeX. Over time, I found myself spending almost as much energy managing my computing environment as I did conducting research. Applications competed for attention, workflows felt fragmented, and the operating system often seemed designed for general-purpose use rather than focused research.

CryptoLab is my attempt to change that.

The long-term vision is a lightweight laboratory tailored specifically for cryptographic research: a system designed around the tools I use most, optimized for experimentation, benchmarking, writing, and learning. At the same time, the project serves another purpose. By building the environment from the ground up, I hope to deepen my understanding of Linux, system customization, and the countless small details that make a computer truly feel like a personal tool.

This journal documents that journey.

Rather than showcasing only polished results, I want to capture the entire process: broken configurations, failed ideas, unexpected discoveries, small victories, and the lessons learned along the way. Building CryptoLab is not just a software project; it is an ongoing exercise in understanding how research, development, and computing environments can fit together into a single coherent workspace.

The Initial Plan

Since flexibility and customization were my primary goals, I chose Arch Linux as the foundation of the project.

Before installing anything, I sketched an initial vision of what the lab should look like. The design centered around a four-panel workstation, with each panel serving a dedicated role in the research workflow.

The upper-left panel would act as the primary workspace. From here I could browse files, open text editors, read papers, or access the web when needed.

The lower-left panel would be dedicated to the terminal. This would be the engine room of the lab, where scripts are executed, programs are compiled, benchmarks are run, and experiments take shape.

The lower-right panel would serve as a performance monitor. Cryptographic research often involves benchmarking implementations and evaluating computational costs, making real-time CPU and memory information an important part of the workflow.

Finally, the upper-right panel would function as a utility dashboard. Depending on the task at hand, it could display notes, reminders, calendars, or other lightweight widgets that support the research process without becoming distractions.

At the time, the idea seemed straightforward: four panels, each with a clear purpose, working together as a unified research environment.

Of course, as I would soon discover, designing the lab was the easy part.

Building it would be an entirely different challenge.

With a rough blueprint in mind and ChatGPT sitting beside me as a guide, I began constructing the first version of CryptoLab.
